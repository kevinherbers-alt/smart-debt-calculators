How this comparison works

Both scenarios assume the same total money leaves your pocket every month: the minimum payment plus your extra amount. The only difference is where the extra goes.

  • Pay debt first: the extra goes toward the debt until it is gone, then the entire monthly amount (minimum + extra) is invested for the remaining years.
  • Invest the extra: you pay only the minimum on the debt and invest the extra from day one.

Net worth at the end of your horizon is investments minus any remaining debt. As a rule of thumb, when your debt's interest rate is higher than your expected market return, paying debt first usually wins; when it is lower, investing usually wins. But the crossover isn't always obvious, which is exactly why running the numbers matters.

What return should I assume?

The long-run average annual return of the U.S. stock market has historically been around 7% after inflation, but past performance never guarantees future results, and returns arrive unevenly. Paying off debt is a guaranteed "return" equal to your interest rate, which is part of why many people split the difference and do some of both. Try a few return assumptions to see how sensitive your answer is.
